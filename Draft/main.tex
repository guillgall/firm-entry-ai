\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The arrival of large language models---most visibly the public release of
ChatGPT in November 2022---has revived interest in how general-purpose
technologies reshape labour markets and firm dynamics
\citep{felten2021occupational, fossen2024new}.
Most of this debate focuses on \emph{workers}: which jobs are at risk, and
for whom.
But a parallel question may be equally consequential: does AI alter the
incentives to \emph{start} a business?

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{fig_fred_motivation}
  \caption{Business applications indexed to January 2020~$=100$ for selected
           sectors. Source: U.S.\ Census Bureau via FRED
           (\url{https://fred.stlouisfed.org/graph/?g=1UHD5}).
           Shaded area indicates the COVID-19 recession (Feb--Apr 2020).}
  \label{fig:fred}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:fred} offers a first look.
After the COVID-19 shock, aggregate business applications surged to historic
highs and have continued rising through 2025.
But the expansion is not uniform.
Professional and Business Services---among the most AI-exposed sectors by
any measure---have pulled sharply away from the aggregate trend.
Manufacturing and Construction, where tasks are less amenable to
language-model automation, have lagged behind.
This heterogeneity motivates the present paper.

Lower entry costs, improved productivity for high-skilled tasks, and
easier access to specialised knowledge may all reduce barriers to
entrepreneurship in AI-exposed sectors \citep{brynjolfsson2018can}.
Conversely, if AI primarily benefits large incumbents, competitive pressures
could \emph{raise} barriers to entry.
Distinguishing between these channels requires variation both across sectors
and over time---variation the public release of ChatGPT conveniently provides.

We use monthly sector-level business application counts from the Census Bureau
Business Formation Statistics \citep{haltiwanger2020bfs} together with the
AI Industry Exposure (AIIE) index of
\citeauthor{felten2021occupational}~(\citeyear{felten2021occupational})
to estimate a difference-in-differences (DiD) model of the form
\begin{equation}
  \log(\text{BA}_{it}) \;=\; \alpha_i + \gamma_t
    + \beta\,(\text{Post}_t \times \text{AI Exposure}_i) + \varepsilon_{it},
  \label{eq:did}
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes 2-digit NAICS sectors, $t$ indexes calendar months,
$\text{Post}_t$ is an indicator equal to one from November 2022 onwards,
$\alpha_i$ are sector fixed effects absorbing time-invariant differences in
application rates, $\gamma_t$ are month fixed effects absorbing aggregate
cyclical variation, and standard errors are clustered by sector.
The coefficient $\beta$ identifies whether sectors with higher AI exposure
experienced a differential change in business formation after ChatGPT.



\paragraph{Related literature.}
\citet{fossen2024new} examine AI adoption and self-employment at the
individual level and find positive effects concentrated among high-skilled
workers.
\citet{chatterji2025transformative} provide a conceptual treatment of how
transformative AI reshapes firm organisation, adoption incentives, and
competitive dynamics---a natural complement to the reduced-form evidence
presented here.
\citet{felten2021occupational} construct the AIOE/AIIE index by combining
O*NET task measures with AI capability scores.
\citet{haltiwanger2020bfs} describe the Census Bureau BFS and demonstrate
its value for studying business dynamism and entry margins.
\citet{brynjolfsson2018can} provide a conceptual framework for mapping
task-level AI capabilities to occupational and industry outcomes.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Business Formation Statistics}
\label{sec:data_bfs}

The Census Bureau BFS provides monthly, seasonally adjusted counts of new
Employer Identification Number (EIN) applications at the 2-digit NAICS
level, derived from IRS Form~SS-4 filings \citep{haltiwanger2020bfs}.
EIN applications are a leading indicator of actual business formation:
roughly half of high-propensity applicants open within eight quarters of
filing.
We use the official seasonally adjusted series directly and index each
sector to January 2020~$= 100$, yielding a panel observed from January 2006 through early~2026.
The BFS publishes 20 sector-level series (excluding the unclassified
``No NAICS'' category): Manufacturing (NAICS 31--33), Retail Trade
(44--45), and Transportation \& Warehousing (48--49) are each published
as single combined codes rather than individual 2-digit breakdowns.
Public Administration (NAICS~92) is present in the BFS but has no
corresponding AIOE score and is therefore excluded from the regression
sample, leaving 19 sectors.
We define two treatment dates: November~2022 (public launch of ChatGPT)
and May~2025 (widespread availability of agentic AI tools).

Figure~\ref{fig:spotlight} plots the index for four selected sectors.
The divergence between Professional Services and goods-producing sectors
is visible from approximately 2021 and widens after November 2022,
consistent with a role for AI in lowering barriers to entry specifically
where tasks are more cognitively intensive.

\subsection{AI Exposure}
\label{sec:data_ai}

Following \citet{felten2021occupational}, we use the AI Industry Exposure
(AIIE) index, which maps occupational AI exposure scores to 4-digit NAICS
industries through BLS employment weights and aggregates to 2-digit NAICS
by employment-weighted averaging.
The index is constructed from 10 AI application domains benchmarked against
O*NET task descriptions and captures the degree to which a sector's
occupational mix is susceptible to AI automation or augmentation.
Finance and Insurance (NAICS~52, AIIE~$= 2.05$), Professional Services
(NAICS~54, AIIE~$= 1.59$), and Information (NAICS~51, AIIE~$= 1.27$)
rank highest; Agriculture (NAICS~11, AIIE~$= -1.56$), Transportation
(NAICS~49, AIIE~$= -1.31$), and Construction (NAICS~23, AIIE~$= -1.00$)
rank lowest.
This cross-sectional variation---which is fixed before the ChatGPT
shock---is the key source of identification.
Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports the AIIE score alongside summary statistics
of the BFS series for each sector in the sample.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:empirical}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

[Empirical strategy placeholder.]

Our baseline specification is equation~\eqref{eq:did}.
The identifying assumption is that, absent the ChatGPT launch, business
applications would have evolved in parallel across sectors regardless of
their AI exposure.
We probe this assumption with an event-study specification that replaces
$\text{Post}_t$ with a full set of month-relative-to-November-2022
indicators interacted with $\text{AI Exposure}_i$.
Parallel pre-trends would be consistent with the identification assumption.

Standard errors are clustered at the 2-digit NAICS sector level
using \texttt{fixest} \citep{berge2018fixest}.

We probe robustness along two dimensions.
First, we substitute the outcome variable with the full suite of BFS
series, which span the entire formation funnel.
On the applications side: corrected business applications (CBA), which
purge filings unlikely to yield an employer business; high-propensity
applications (HBA); and applications with planned wages (WBA).
On the formations side: businesses actually formed within four quarters
(BF4Q) and eight quarters (BF8Q) of filing; Census-projected formations
at both horizons (PBF4Q, PBF8Q); and seasonally-adjusted formation
counts (SBF4Q, SBF8Q).
Finally, the median number of weeks from application to formation
(DUR4Q, DUR8Q) provides a complementary timing dimension: a negative
coefficient would indicate that AI exposure compresses the
application-to-opening lag, consistent with lower operational setup costs.
If AI lowers barriers to entry, the effect should be visible across all
stages of the formation funnel.
Second, we re-estimate the model at the state level, replacing sector
fixed effects with state fixed effects.
The outcome is the log of total seasonally adjusted business applications
aggregated across all sectors within a state, and the AI exposure measure
is the AI Geographic Exposure (AIGE) index from
\citeauthor{felten2021occupational}~(\citeyear{felten2021occupational})
Appendix~C, which is constructed directly from the occupational mix of each
state and its workers' AI exposure.
This specification absorbs permanent state-level differences in
entrepreneurial activity and exploits cross-state variation in AI exposure
that is independent of the sector composition used in the main analysis.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Descriptive Evidence}
\label{sec:results_desc}

Figure~\ref{fig:desc} plots mean indexed business applications by AI
exposure quartile.
Prior to 2020 the four quartile groups track closely together, consistent
with parallel pre-trends.
After the COVID-19 surge and recovery, the top quartile (high AI exposure)
diverges upward, while the bottom quartile remains below the aggregate
trend.
The gap is most pronounced from 2024 onwards, consistent with a gradual
diffusion of AI tools into entrepreneurial activity rather than an
immediate discrete response.

\subsection{Event Study}
\label{sec:results_es}

Figure~\ref{fig:es} presents the event-study coefficients.
The pre-period estimates ($t < 0$) are small and statistically
indistinguishable from zero, supporting the parallel-trends assumption.
Post-launch, coefficients rise gradually before the 95\,\% confidence
interval clears zero around 2025 ($t \approx +25$ to $+30$), indicating
that the differential effect of AI exposure on business formation became
statistically significant roughly two to three years after the ChatGPT
launch.
This pattern is consistent with a diffusion mechanism: entrepreneurs
require time to learn the tools, identify viable business models, and
translate that into actual EIN filings.
A visible second inflection around $t = +30$ (May~2025, the emergence of
widely available agentic AI tools) suggests a possible amplification of
the initial shock, which the two-period DiD in column~(4) of
Table~\ref{tab:did} is designed to capture.

\subsection{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:results_robust}

\subsubsection*{Alternative BFS series}

Table~\ref{tab:robust} replicates the baseline DiD specification
(column~(1) of Table~\ref{tab:did}) using three alternative BFS outcome
variables.
Column~(2) replaces total business applications with high-propensity
applications (HBA), defined by the Census Bureau as those most likely to
become active employer businesses.
Column~(3) uses applications with planned wages (WBA), which signal
explicit intent to hire at the time of EIN filing.
Column~(4) uses the count of businesses that actually formed within four
quarters of application (BF4Q), the realised formation margin.
A consistent pattern across the formation funnel---from intent (WBA) to
high-quality applications (HBA) to actual formations (BF4Q)---strengthens
the interpretation that AI exposure shifted both the quantity and quality
of entrepreneurial activity, not merely the propensity to file.
We additionally examine formations within eight quarters (BF8Q) in the
event-study figures; because the Census requires a full eight-quarter
follow-up window before publishing BF8Q, the series is right-truncated
approximately two years before the data vintage and does not have
sufficient post-period coverage for a clean DiD estimate.
The event-study coefficients are nevertheless informative: comparing the
BF4Q and BF8Q panels reveals whether the additional entry in
high-AI-exposure sectors converts to operating businesses quickly or
gradually.

Figure~\ref{fig:es_robust} presents the corresponding event-study
coefficients for all four series.
The pre-period estimates are small and insignificant across every panel,
ruling out differential pre-trends.
Post-launch dynamics are qualitatively consistent: coefficients drift
upward at similar timing and reach statistical significance in the same
window regardless of which formation margin is used as the outcome.
The BF4Q panel is shifted rightward relative to applications panels,
reflecting the mechanical lag between filing and actual business opening,
and provides perhaps the cleanest evidence that the effect translates into
realised entry rather than merely intent.

\subsubsection*{State-level panel with state fixed effects}

Table~\ref{tab:state} presents the state-level DiD estimates.
The unit of observation is now a state-month cell, with the outcome being
log total seasonally adjusted business applications aggregated across all
sectors within the state.
The AI exposure measure is the AIGE index of
\citeauthor{felten2021occupational}~(\citeyear{felten2021occupational}),
which captures the AI exposure of a state's workforce through its
occupational mix.
State fixed effects absorb permanent cross-state differences in
entrepreneurial culture, regulatory environment, and industrial composition;
month fixed effects absorb national macro shocks as before.
Standard errors are clustered at the state level (56 clusters).

Figure~\ref{fig:desc_state} provides raw descriptive evidence at the state
level, analogous to Figure~\ref{fig:desc} for sectors.
States are sorted into quartiles by their AIGE index and the cross-state
mean of indexed business applications (January~2020\,=\,100) is plotted for
each quartile.

Figure~\ref{fig:es_state} shows the corresponding event-study coefficients,
and Figure~\ref{fig:es_state_facet} repeats the exercise across the four
BFS series published at the state level (BA, HBA, WBA, CBA; formation
series are national-only).
Pre-period coefficients are flat across all panels, consistent with
parallel pre-trends, but the post-launch coefficients are imprecise and
do not reach conventional significance levels in any series.

The absence of a detectable state-level effect likely reflects two
compounding limitations.
First, states are highly diversified: even a state with a high-AIGE
workforce will contain many low-AI sectors that dilute the aggregate
application signal.
The sector-level design exploits within-sector variation in AI exposure
that is averaged away when outcomes are aggregated to the state level.
Second, the cross-state dispersion of AIGE is considerably smaller than
the cross-sector dispersion of AIOE, reducing statistical power.
Together, these features make the state-level panel a demanding test;
the sector-level estimates remain the primary evidence.
