\begin{abstract}
  \noindent
I study the relationship between sectoral AI exposure and firm entry in the United States. 
Using monthly business application data from the Census Bureau Business Formation Statistics and the AI Industry Exposure index of \citet{felten2021occupational}, I document an association between a sector's AI exposure and its post-2022 business application growth. 
The association is not immediate: the effect emerges only tentatively in early 2023 before fading, then re-emerges persistently and grows monotonically from mid-2024 onwards, broadly coinciding with the diffusion and broader adoption of AI tools. 
While I cannot rule out confounding from other sector-specific trends, this dynamic pattern is consistent with AI progressively lowering entry costs or raising entrepreneurial returns in exposed sectors.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

After the COVID-19 shock, aggregate business applications surged \citep{decker2023surging}. The release of ChatGPT in November-2022 seems to have increased firm entry, too, especially from 2024 onwards, as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:spotlight}. 

This expansion is not uniform across sectors: industries such as Professional Services have increased more than the average, while others such as Construction have lagged behind. From this plot a simple observation arises: while the former sector is exposed to AI, the latter is less so. Does this pattern hold across all sectors, though? 

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{fig_spotlight}
  \caption{Business applications indexed to November 2022~$=100$. Professional Services (NAICS~54), Total (all NAICS), and Construction (NAICS~23). Seasonally adjusted.}
  \label{fig:spotlight}
\end{figure}

In this paper I show that it does: AI-exposed sectors have experienced a firm entry boom, while less-exposed sectors have lagged behind.

I also study the timing of this relationship: I show that it held temporarily in early 2023, but then more persistently from 2024 onwards. 



\paragraph{Contribution to the literature}

Most of the ``economics of AI" debate focuses on workers: which jobs are at risk, and
for whom.
But a parallel question may be equally consequential: does AI alter the
incentives to start a business?

\citet{chatterji2025transformative} provide a conceptual treatment of how
transformative AI reshapes firm organisation, adoption incentives, and
competitive dynamics---a natural complement to the reduced-form evidence
presented here.
\citet{felten2021occupational} construct the AIOE/AIIE index by combining
O*NET task measures with AI capability scores. In future iterations of this paper, I plan to include other AI exposure metrics, such as the LLM-based occupational exposure scores developed by \citet{eloundou2024gpts}.

I combine the AI literature with the firm entry dimension, by using business formation data as in \citet{decker2023surging}, and papers cited therein.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Business Formation Statistics}
\label{sec:data_bfs}

The Census Bureau BFS provides monthly, seasonally adjusted counts of new
Employer Identification Number (EIN) applications at the 2-digit NAICS
level, derived from IRS Form~SS-4 filings.
EIN applications are a leading indicator of actual business formation \citep{decker2023surging}.

\subsection{AI Exposure}
\label{sec:data_ai}

Following \citet{felten2021occupational}, I use the AI Industry Exposure
(AIIE) index, which maps occupational AI exposure scores to 4-digit NAICS
industries through BLS employment weights.
I aggregate to 2-digit and 2-digit NAICS using employment-weighted averaging,
with 2019 BLS OES industry employment counts as weights, consistent
with \citet{felten2021occupational} own use of 2019 employment in constructing the index.\footnote{Results are unchanged when using an unweighted mean across 4-digit sub-industries.}
Table~\ref{tab:summary} in Appendix~\ref{app:summary} reports the AIIE 2-digit score alongside summary statistics
of the BFS series for each sector in the sample.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
I first focus on 2-digit NAICS industries.

\subsection{Regression}
\label{sec:results_desc}

The scatter-plot in Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} illustrates the cross-sectional relationship clearly.
Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} plots the percentage change in business
applications from November~2022 to the most recent month available
against each sector's AI exposure.


\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig_scatter}
  \caption{AI exposure versus change in business applications by 2-digit NAICS sector.}
  \label{fig:scatter}
\end{figure}

\input{tab_scatter.tex}





Table~\ref{tab:scatter} formalises the scatter with a cross-sectional
regression of the form
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\overline{\text{BA}}_{i} \;=\; \alpha + \delta\,\text{AIIE}_i + \varepsilon_i,
  \label{eq:scatter}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\overline{\text{BA}}_{i}$ is the percentage change in business applications
from November~2022 to the most recent month available for sector~$i$, and $\text{AIIE}_i$
is the normalised AI Industry Exposure index.
Column~(1) is unweighted OLS; column~(2) weights observations by
pre-period mean business applications, giving larger sectors proportionally
more influence.
The slope is positive and statistically significant in both specifications,
and the weighted estimate is somewhat larger, indicating that the positive
relationship between AI exposure and application growth is stronger
among economically larger sectors.

\subsection{Difference-in-Differences}
\label{sec:results_did}


I estimate the effect of AI exposure ($\text{AIIE}_{i}$) on business applications ($\text{BA}_{it}$) using a difference-in-differences (DiD) model of the form
\begin{equation}
  \log(\text{BA}_{it}) \;=\; \alpha_i + \gamma_t
    + \beta\,(\text{Post}_t \times \text{AIIE}_i) + \varepsilon_{it},
  \label{eq:did}
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes 2-digit NAICS sectors, $t$ indexes calendar months,
$\text{Post}_t$ is an indicator equal to one from November 2022 onwards,
$\alpha_i$ are sector fixed effects absorbing time-invariant differences in
application rates, $\gamma_t$ are month fixed effects absorbing aggregate
cyclical variation, and standard errors are clustered by sector.
The coefficient $\beta$ identifies whether sectors with higher AI exposure
experienced a differential change in business formation after ChatGPT.


Table~\ref{tab:did} (Appendix~\ref{app:did}) reports the DiD estimates.
All three columns pool the entire post-November~2022 period into a single
\textit{Post} indicator, with columns~(1)--(3) using log business applications,
the indexed BA outcome, and raw BA levels respectively.
To translate the coefficient into economically interpretable magnitudes,
Table~\ref{tab:mfx} (Appendix~\ref{app:did}) reports the implied percentage difference in business
applications relative to a sector at the 25th percentile of AI exposure,
evaluated at selected percentiles of the AIOE distribution.

The pooled \textit{Post} coefficient is statistically insignificant because the treatment effect is time-varying: pooling the full post-November~2022 window into a single indicator averages near-zero early-period effects with the large, persistent effects that only emerge from mid-2024 onwards, attenuating the estimate toward zero. The event study below makes this dynamic explicit.

\subsection{Event Study}
\label{sec:results_es}

Figure~\ref{fig:es} presents the event-study coefficients.
The pre-period estimates ($t < 0$) are small and statistically
indistinguishable from zero, supporting the parallel-trends assumption.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig_event_study}
  \caption{Event-study estimates: effect of AI exposure on
           $\log(\text{Business Applications})$ relative to
           November 2022 ($t = 0$). Shaded band is the 95\,\% confidence
           interval based on sector-clustered standard errors.}
  \label{fig:es}
\end{figure}

A handful of coefficients are statistically significant in the first
months immediately following the ChatGPT release, suggesting an early, tentative response.
These effects then recede into insignificance across roughly a year, before re-emerging persistently from
$t = +17$ (April~2024, $p < 0.10$) and $t = +18$ (May~2024, $p < 0.05$)
onward.
From May~2024, every subsequent monthly coefficient is statistically
significant at the 5\,\% level or better, and point estimates grow
monotonically through the end of the sample, reaching $0.48$ at
$t = +36$.

\section{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:results_robust}

\subsection{Alternative Business Applications Metrics}
\label{sec:ba_robust}

I probe robustness by substituting the outcome with the full suite of available BFS series spanning the formation funnel. 

On the applications side, I use corrected business applications (CBA), which purge filings unlikely to yield an employer business; high-propensity applications (HBA), which restrict to applicants with a high predicted probability of hiring; and applications with planned wages (WBA), indicating explicit intent to hire. 

On the formations side, I use seasonally-adjusted formations within four and eight quarters (SBF4Q, SBF8Q). The realised formation counts (BF4Q, BF8Q) are excluded as the Census has not yet published these beyond 2022 due to publication lags.

Figure~\ref{fig:es_robust} presents the corresponding event-study
coefficients for all six series.
The pre-period estimates are small and insignificant across every panel,
ruling out differential pre-trends.
Post-launch dynamics are qualitatively consistent: coefficients drift
upward at similar timing and reach statistical significance in the same
window regardless of which outcome is used.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_es_robust}
  \caption{Event-study estimates for six BFS series.
           Dashed vertical line marks November 2022 ($t=0$).}
  \label{fig:es_robust}
\end{figure}

The corresponding pooled DiD estimates across all six series are reported in Table~\ref{tab:robust} (Appendix~\ref{app:did}); as with the baseline, the pooled coefficients are statistically insignificant, consistent with the time-varying nature of the effect documented in the event study.

\subsection{3-digit NAICS}
\label{sec:3digit_robust}

Figure~\ref{fig:scatter_naics3} replicates the motivating scatter at the
finer 3-digit NAICS level, comparing mean weekly business applications in
November~2022 with November~2025 across 75 sectors.
The positive cross-sectional gradient visible at the 2-digit level
(Figure~\ref{fig:scatter}) persists at higher disaggregation, and the
bubble sizes — proportional to pre-period application volume — make clear
that larger sectors tend to sit higher on the fitted line.
Table~\ref{tab:scatter_naics3} formalises this.
The unweighted OLS slope is near zero and statistically insignificant
($\hat{\delta} = 0.9$, $p > 0.10$, $R^2 \approx 0.00$), reflecting
substantial noise among the many small 3-digit sectors.
Once sectors are weighted by their pre-period application volume, however,
the slope rises sharply to $41.1$ ($p < 0.01$, $R^2 = 0.31$): among
the sectors that account for the bulk of economic activity, higher AI
exposure is strongly associated with faster application growth.

Figure~\ref{fig:es_naics3} presents the event-study coefficients from
estimating equation~\eqref{eq:did} on the weekly 3-digit NAICS panel.
Because the raw weekly series exhibit strong sector-specific seasonality
— high-AIOE sectors such as Professional Services have a markedly
different within-year cycle than low-AIOE sectors such as Construction —
each sector's weekly BA series is first seasonally adjusted using STL
decomposition on the log scale (multiplicative, periodic window) before
entering the regression.
The event study uses 4-week bins, sector and ISO year-week fixed effects,
and observations weighted by each sector's pre-period mean weekly BA.
Weighting by sector size is motivated by the cross-sectional evidence in
Table~\ref{tab:scatter_naics3}: the relationship between AI exposure and
application growth is concentrated among larger sectors, so weighting
ensures that identification comes from the same margin.
The pre-period coefficients are small and statistically indistinguishable
from zero, supporting the parallel-trends assumption at finer sectoral
disaggregation.
Post-launch dynamics mirror those in the 2-digit baseline: coefficients
drift upward and eventually reach statistical significance, consistent
with AI-exposed sectors experiencing a relative surge in weekly business
applications following the ChatGPT release.

\subsection{US States}
\label{sec:states_robust}

Appendix~\ref{app:state} reports a state-level robustness check using the
AI Geographic Exposure (AIGE) index of
\citeauthor{felten2021occupational}~(\citeyear{felten2021occupational})
and state $\times$ month panels.
The state-level estimates are statistically insignificant, likely because
state aggregation dilutes the within-sector AI variation that drives the
main results; the sector-level estimates remain the primary evidence.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper documents a positive relationship between sectoral AI exposure and firm entry in the United States.
Using monthly business application data from the Census Bureau Business Formation Statistics and the AI Industry Exposure index of \citet{felten2021occupational}, I find that industries with higher AI exposure have experienced markedly stronger business application growth following the release of ChatGPT in November~2022.

The cross-sectional evidence is clear: Table~\ref{tab:scatter} shows that a one-standard-deviation increase in AI Industry Exposure is associated with a 46--55 percentage-point increase in business applications since November~2022 (unweighted OLS: $\hat{\delta}=54.9$, $p<0.01$; WLS: $\hat{\delta}=45.7$, $p<0.01$), and this relationship is robust to weighting by sector size and to disaggregation to the 3-digit NAICS level.
The dynamic evidence, however, is more informative than the cross-section alone.
The association is not immediate: effects emerge tentatively in early~2023 before fading, then re-emerge persistently and grow monotonically from mid-2024 onwards.
This two-phase pattern---a brief early response, a quiescent middle period, and a durable late surge---broadly coincides with the diffusion and broader adoption of AI tools beyond early adopters, and is consistent with AI progressively lowering entry costs or raising entrepreneurial returns in exposed sectors.

The pooled difference-in-differences estimate is statistically insignificant precisely because it averages near-zero early-period effects with the large effects that materialise only from mid-2024.
The event study, which lets the coefficient vary month by month, clarifies the picture: from May~2024, every monthly coefficient is statistically significant at the 5\% level, and point estimates continue to widen through the end of the sample in early~2026.

While I cannot rule out confounding from other sector-specific trends that happened to accelerate in high-exposure industries from mid-2024 onwards, the robustness of the pattern across alternative business-application series, across NAICS disaggregation levels, and across estimation approaches makes a purely coincidental explanation less compelling.

Several directions remain for future work.
Linking individual firm data to AI exposure measures would allow me to study the characteristics of new entrants in AI-exposed sectors and to distinguish among the mechanisms---lower entry costs, reduced informational barriers, or higher expected returns.
Incorporating alternative AI exposure measures \citep{eloundou2024gpts} would further strengthen robustness.
Extending the analysis to other countries would shed light on whether the US pattern reflects idiosyncratic institutional features or a broader regularity in how general-purpose technologies reshape the incentives to start a business.

\section{Event Study via Cross-Sectional Regressions}
\label{app:es_regs}

As a robustness check on the DiD event study in
Section~\ref{sec:results_es}, I re-estimate the dynamic effect of AI
exposure on business applications without the two-way fixed-effects
structure, running instead a separate cross-sectional regression for
each calendar month:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\log\text{BA}_{i,t} \;=\; \alpha_t + \beta_t\,\text{AIIE}_i
                              + \varepsilon_{i,t},
  \label{eq:es_regs}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\log\text{BA}_{i,t} = \log(\text{BA}_{i,t}) -
\log(\text{BA}_{i,\text{Nov 2022}})$ is the log-change in business
applications for sector~$i$ between month~$t$ and the November~2022
baseline, and $\text{AIIE}_i$ is the normalised AI Industry Exposure
index. Each of the 19 two-digit NAICS sectors contributes one
observation per regression; November~2022 is the omitted reference with
$\widehat{\beta}_t = 0$ by construction. Standard errors are HC1
heteroskedasticity-robust.

The coefficient path $\{\widehat{\beta}_t\}$, plotted in
Figure~\ref{fig:es_regs}, is the analogue of a standard event-study
but estimated without pooling across months and without imposing
sector or month fixed effects: identification comes purely from the
cross-sectional gradient between AI exposure and application growth
at each horizon. The pre-period estimates cluster tightly around zero,
and the post-launch dynamics closely track those of the DiD event study
in Figure~\ref{fig:es}: a short-lived response in early~2023, a flat
period through late~2023, and a persistently significant, monotonically
widening gap from mid-2024 through the end of the sample in
March~2026.

\section{State-Level Robustness}
\label{app:state}

Table~\ref{tab:state} presents the state-level DiD estimates.
The unit of observation is a state-month cell, with the outcome being
log total seasonally adjusted business applications aggregated across all
sectors within the state.
The AI exposure measure is the AIGE index of
\citeauthor{felten2021occupational}~(\citeyear{felten2021occupational}),
which captures the AI exposure of a state's workforce through its
occupational mix.
State fixed effects absorb permanent cross-state differences in
entrepreneurial culture, regulatory environment, and industrial composition;
month fixed effects absorb national macro shocks as before.
Standard errors are clustered at the state level (56 clusters).

Figure~\ref{fig:desc_state} provides raw descriptive evidence at the state level.
States are sorted into quartiles by their AIGE index and the cross-state
mean of indexed business applications (January~2020\,=\,100) is plotted for
each quartile.

Figure~\ref{fig:es_state} shows the corresponding event-study coefficients,
and Figure~\ref{fig:es_state_facet} repeats the exercise across the four
BFS series published at the state level (BA, HBA, WBA, CBA; formation
series are national-only).
Pre-period coefficients are flat across all panels, consistent with
parallel pre-trends, but the post-launch coefficients are imprecise and
do not reach conventional significance levels in any series.

The absence of a detectable state-level effect likely reflects two
compounding limitations.
First, states are highly diversified: even a state with a high-AIGE
workforce will contain many low-AI sectors that dilute the aggregate
application signal.
The sector-level design exploits within-sector variation in AI exposure
that is averaged away when outcomes are aggregated to the state level.
Second, the cross-state dispersion of AIGE is considerably smaller than
the cross-sector dispersion of AIOE, reducing statistical power.
Together, these features make the state-level panel a demanding test;
the sector-level estimates remain the primary evidence.
