\begin{abstract}
  \noindent
In this short paper, I show that, following the release of ChatGPT in November 2022, firm entry increased in AI-exposed sectors, temporarily in early 2023 and then more persistently from 2024 onwards. Using monthly sector-level business application data from the Census Bureau Business Formation Statistics, I find that the effect is [X]\% larger for a sector at the 75th versus 25th percentile of AI exposure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

After the COVID-19 shock, aggregate business applications surged to historic
highs \citep{decker2023surging} and have continued rising through 2026, but the expansion is not
uniform across sectors, as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:spotlight}. The release of ChatGPT in November-2022 seems to have increased firm entry, too. However, this surge 

Sectors such as Professional Services have increased more than the average, while others such as Construction have lagged behind. From this plot a simple observation arises: while the former sector is exposed to AI, the latter ones are less so. Does this pattern hold across all sectors, though? 

In this paper I show that it does: AI-exposed sectors have experienced a firm entry boom, while less-exposed sectors have lagged behind. The scatter-plot in Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} illustrates this clearly.

\paragraph{Contribution to the literature}

Most of the ``economics of AI" debate focuses on workers: which jobs are at risk, and
for whom.
But a parallel question may be equally consequential: does AI alter the
incentives to start a business?

\citet{chatterji2025transformative} provide a conceptual treatment of how
transformative AI reshapes firm organisation, adoption incentives, and
competitive dynamics---a natural complement to the reduced-form evidence
presented here.
\citet{felten2021occupational} construct the AIOE/AIIE index by combining
O*NET task measures with AI capability scores.

I combine the AI literature with the firm entry dimension, by using business formation data as in \citet{decker2023surging}, and papers cited therein.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Business Formation Statistics}
\label{sec:data_bfs}

The Census Bureau BFS provides monthly, seasonally adjusted counts of new
Employer Identification Number (EIN) applications at the 2-digit NAICS
level, derived from IRS Form~SS-4 filings.
EIN applications are a leading indicator of actual business formation \citep{decker2023surging}.

\subsection{AI Exposure}
\label{sec:data_ai}

Following \citet{felten2021occupational}, we use the AI Industry Exposure
(AIIE) index, which maps occupational AI exposure scores to 4-digit NAICS
industries through BLS employment weights.
We aggregate to 2-digit NAICS using employment-weighted averaging,
with 2019 BLS OES industry employment counts as weights, consistent
with Felten et al.'s own use of 2019 employment in constructing the index.\footnote{Results are unchanged when using an unweighted mean across 4-digit sub-industries.}
Table~\ref{tab:summary} in Appendix~\ref{app:summary} reports the AIIE score alongside summary statistics
of the BFS series for each sector in the sample.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Regression}
\label{sec:results_desc}

Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} plots the percentage change in business
applications from November~2022 to the most recent month available
against each sector's AI exposure.



Table~\ref{tab:scatter} formalises the scatter with a cross-sectional
regression of the form
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\overline{\text{BA}}_{i} \;=\; \alpha + \delta\,\text{AIIE}_i + \varepsilon_i,
  \label{eq:scatter}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\overline{\text{BA}}_{i}$ is the percentage change in business applications
from November~2022 to the most recent month available for sector~$i$, and $\text{AIIE}_i$
is the normalised AI Industry Exposure index.
Column~(1) is unweighted OLS; column~(2) weights observations by
pre-period mean business applications, giving larger sectors proportionally
more influence.
The slope is positive and statistically significant in both specifications,
and the weighted estimate is somewhat larger, indicating that the positive
relationship between AI exposure and application growth is stronger
among economically larger sectors.

\subsection{Difference-in-Differences}
\label{sec:results_did}


I estimate the effect of AI exposure ($\text{AIIE}_{i}$) on business applicacations ($\text{BA}_{it}$) using a difference-in-differences (DiD) model of the form
\begin{equation}
  \log(\text{BA}_{it}) \;=\; \alpha_i + \gamma_t
    + \beta\,(\text{Post}_t \times \text{AIIE}_i) + \varepsilon_{it},
  \label{eq:did}
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes 2-digit NAICS sectors, $t$ indexes calendar months,
$\text{Post}_t$ is an indicator equal to one from November 2022 onwards,
$\alpha_i$ are sector fixed effects absorbing time-invariant differences in
application rates, $\gamma_t$ are month fixed effects absorbing aggregate
cyclical variation, and standard errors are clustered by sector.
The coefficient $\beta$ identifies whether sectors with higher AI exposure
experienced a differential change in business formation after ChatGPT.

Table~\ref{tab:did} reports the DiD estimates.
All three columns pool the entire post-November~2022 period into a single
\textit{Post} indicator, with columns~(1)--(3) using log business applications,
the indexed BA outcome, and raw BA levels respectively.

\input{tab_did.tex}

To translate the coefficient into economically interpretable magnitudes,
Table~\ref{tab:mfx} reports the implied percentage difference in business
applications relative to a sector at the 25th percentile of AI exposure,
evaluated at selected percentiles of the AIOE distribution.
A sector at the median of AI exposure is estimated to have had
2.3\% more applications than a P25 sector in the post-ChatGPT period;
the gap widens to 5.1\% at the 75th percentile and 10.7\% at the 90th.

\subsection{Event Study}
\label{sec:results_es}

Figure~\ref{fig:es} presents the event-study coefficients.
The pre-period estimates ($t < 0$) are small and statistically
indistinguishable from zero, supporting the parallel-trends assumption.

A handful of coefficients are statistically significant in the first
months immediately following the ChatGPT release, suggesting an early, tentative response.
These effects then recede into insignificance across roughly a year, before re-emerging persistently from
$t = +17$ (April~2024, $p < 0.10$) and $t = +18$ (May~2024, $p < 0.05$)
onward.
From May~2024, every subsequent monthly coefficient is statistically
significant at the 5\,\% level or better, and point estimates grow
monotonically through the end of the sample, reaching $0.48$ at
$t = +36$.

\section{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:results_robust}

\subsection{Alternative Business Applications Metrics}
\label{sec:ba_robust}

We probe robustness by substituting the outcome with the full suite of available BFS series spanning the formation funnel. 

On the applications side, we use corrected business applications (CBA), which purge filings unlikely to yield an employer business; high-propensity applications (HBA), which restrict to applicants with a high predicted probability of hiring; and applications with planned wages (WBA), indicating explicit intent to hire. 

On the formations side, we use seasonally-adjusted formations within four and eight quarters (SBF4Q, SBF8Q). The realised formation counts (BF4Q, BF8Q) are excluded as the Census has not yet published these beyond 2022 due to publication lags.

Figure~\ref{fig:es_robust} presents the corresponding event-study
coefficients for all six series.
The pre-period estimates are small and insignificant across every panel,
ruling out differential pre-trends.
Post-launch dynamics are qualitatively consistent: coefficients drift
upward at similar timing and reach statistical significance in the same
window regardless of which outcome is used.

\subsection{3-digit NAICS}
\label{sec:3digit_robust}

Figure~\ref{fig:scatter_naics3} replicates the motivating scatter at the
finer 3-digit NAICS level, comparing mean weekly business applications in
November~2022 with November~2025 across 75 sectors.
The positive cross-sectional gradient visible at the 2-digit level
(Figure~\ref{fig:scatter}) persists at higher disaggregation, and the
bubble sizes — proportional to pre-period application volume — make clear
that larger sectors tend to sit higher on the fitted line.
Table~\ref{tab:scatter_naics3} formalises this.
The unweighted OLS slope is near zero and statistically insignificant
($\hat{\delta} = 0.9$, $p > 0.10$, $R^2 \approx 0.00$), reflecting
substantial noise among the many small 3-digit sectors.
Once sectors are weighted by their pre-period application volume, however,
the slope rises sharply to $41.1$ ($p < 0.01$, $R^2 = 0.31$): among
the sectors that account for the bulk of economic activity, higher AI
exposure is strongly associated with faster application growth.

The state-level estimates are statistically insignificant, likely because
state aggregation dilutes the within-sector AI variation that drives the
main results; the sector-level estimates remain the primary evidence.

\section{State-Level Robustness}
\label{app:state}

Table~\ref{tab:state} presents the state-level DiD estimates.
The unit of observation is a state-month cell, with the outcome being
log total seasonally adjusted business applications aggregated across all
sectors within the state.
The AI exposure measure is the AIGE index of
\citeauthor{felten2021occupational}~(\citeyear{felten2021occupational}),
which captures the AI exposure of a state's workforce through its
occupational mix.
State fixed effects absorb permanent cross-state differences in
entrepreneurial culture, regulatory environment, and industrial composition;
month fixed effects absorb national macro shocks as before.
Standard errors are clustered at the state level (56 clusters).

Figure~\ref{fig:desc_state} provides raw descriptive evidence at the state
level, analogous to Figure~\ref{fig:desc} for sectors.
States are sorted into quartiles by their AIGE index and the cross-state
mean of indexed business applications (January~2020\,=\,100) is plotted for
each quartile.

\input{tab_state.tex}

Figure~\ref{fig:es_state} shows the corresponding event-study coefficients,
and Figure~\ref{fig:es_state_facet} repeats the exercise across the four
BFS series published at the state level (BA, HBA, WBA, CBA; formation
series are national-only).
Pre-period coefficients are flat across all panels, consistent with
parallel pre-trends, but the post-launch coefficients are imprecise and
do not reach conventional significance levels in any series.

The absence of a detectable state-level effect likely reflects two
compounding limitations.
First, states are highly diversified: even a state with a high-AIGE
workforce will contain many low-AI sectors that dilute the aggregate
application signal.
The sector-level design exploits within-sector variation in AI exposure
that is averaged away when outcomes are aggregated to the state level.
Second, the cross-state dispersion of AIGE is considerably smaller than
the cross-sector dispersion of AIOE, reducing statistical power.
Together, these features make the state-level panel a demanding test;
the sector-level estimates remain the primary evidence.
